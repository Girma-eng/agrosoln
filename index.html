Supply Gap

The Supply Gap relates to the inadequate supply of essential technologies, inputs, and services to farmers.

  • Limited access to mechanisation, quality seeds, fertilizers, irrigation systems, and innovative tools prevents smallholders from improving productivity and competitiveness.
  • dditionally, fragmented supply chains and poor last-mile delivery systems exacerbate the challenge, keeping critical technologies and inputs out of farmers' reach.

The Capacity Gap

  • The Capacity Gap represents a critical barrier for Ethiopia’s smallholder farmers, who often lack the skills, knowledge, and resources needed to adopt modern farming practices and improve productivity.
  • Limited access to training, extension services, and financial support prevents farmers from utilising technologies, quality inputs, and sustainable practices.
  • Low digital literacy and market awareness further restrict their ability to engage with emerging tools and off-taker markets.
  • Addressing the gap requires comprehensive capacity development programs—covering areas such as mechanisation, regenerative agricultural practices, financial management, and market access—are essential to empower farmers.

Why Mechanization

Agricultural Mechanization?

Agricultural mechanization is broadly defined as the application of tools, implements and powered machinery and equipment to achieve agricultural production. It encompasses all levels of farming technology from simple hand tools to advanced automated systems and smart farming technologies.

Mechanization represents a fundamental transformation in how we produce food. It's not merely about replacing manual labor, but about reimagining agricultural processes to meet the challenges of our time.

The transition to mechanized farming enables us to address critical issues of food security, labor shortages, and environmental sustainability while meeting the demands of a growing global population.

"Mechanization bridges the gap between traditional farming practices and the technological innovations needed to sustainably feed our future."

Sustainable agricultural mechanization (SAM)

Sustainable agricultural mechanization (SAM) focuses on using machinery in farming in a way that is economically viable, environmentally responsible, and socially beneficial, contributing to the long-term health of the agricultural sector and food systems. Key Considerations:

Technological:

  • Choosing appropriate machinery and technologies that are efficient and effective.

Economic:

  • Ensuring that mechanization is affordable and financially viable for farmers and other stakeholders.

Social

  • Considering the impact on rural communities, labor practices, and social equity.

Environmental

  • Minimizing negative impacts on the environment, such as soil degradation, water pollution, and greenhouse gas emissions.

Cultural:

  • Respecting local traditions and practices.

Business Model Devlopment

As part of our transformative journey, we organized a workshop aimed at co-creating a business model for agricultural mechanization. The workshop brought together a diverse group of participants, including service providers, farmers, engineers, agricultural experts, and bank representatives, to collaborate on designing a sustainable and effective business model. During the workshop, we provided comprehensive training on the business model canvas, empowering participants with the tools and knowledge needed to develop innovative and viable strategies for agricultural mechanization. This effort ensured that the business model created reflects the insights and expertise of various stakeholders, leading to a more inclusive and impactful approach to agricultural development.

Understanding the problem—the existing mechanization system and gap analysis

The survey aimed to understand the problem—the existing mechanization system and gap analysis by assessing existing equipment, infrastructure, personnel, business models, and training facilities of mechanization service providers in differnt parts of the country. The scope included completing a comprehensive gap analysis and developing customized plans to address shortfalls in equipment, infrastructure, personnel, and training. The survey results highlighted common challenges faced by mechanization service providers in Ethiopia, including the unavailability of spare parts, financial constraints, limited maintenance services, and high fuel costs. By addressing these challenges and implementing the customized plans developed, the aim was to improve the effectiveness and efficiency of mechanization services provided to farmers in the surveyed areas. Ultimately, the outcome seeks to enhance agricultural practices, increase productivity, and contribute to the overall development of the region's agriculture sector.

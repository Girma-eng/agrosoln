    01-The Context

    Smallholder Farmers are the Front Line…

    Smallholder farmers are the foundation of Africa's food system —yet they're the least supported. They're expected to grow food for the continent with no infrastructure, no finance, no market visibility, and no reliable services.

    This as a result of various structural problems and their effects
    • Manual farming →Low productivity
    • Informal markets → Unstable prices
    • No access to finance → No way to upgrade/grow
    • Highly climate-exposed → No buffers
    If we can't support the needs at the base of the system, nothing above it will function.

    01-The Context

    The Ecosystem struggles to support the Farmers…

    The supply chain of actors meant to support these farmers — mechanisation service providers, input distributors, aggregators, buyers — are fragmented, undercapitalised, and operating without standardisation or scale . They also face structural problems that limit their ability to create impact and support the system:

    • Services are fragmented→ Informal and often donor-dependent
    • No interoperable platform→ No standard business model to scale
    • No risk pooling→ No systemic way to pool risk or create shared infrastructure
    • Ad hoc markets→ Markets are spotty, and aggregation is ad hoc
    This service ecosystem is the engine room for scale — but everyone is building in isolation → no compounding value.

    01-The Context

    Government struggles to support the Ecosystem

    Governments are expected to deliver food security, rural job creation, and climate resilience. But they're trapped managing an informal sector that's hard to track, fund, or govern.

    • Inefficient investment→ Most public programs lack durability and fail to create systemic change
    • Siloed data→ Can't design or adapt policy fast enough without integrated systems
    • Foreign input dependence→ Over-reliance on imports leads to loss of food sovereignty
    • Pilot overload→ Too many isolated initiatives, not enough systemic solutions
    Governments need investment-ready systems they can trust and align with — not more one-off projects.

    02 — The System Vision

    Level 1: Farmhub Field Station

    Structuring & Servicing the Last Mile

    A local service hub that provides smallholder farmers with access to the core infrastructure they need to close the yield, income, and market gap.
    Each hub is a physical location — franchised and locally operated — offering affordable, standardised access:
    • Mechanisation-as-a-service
    • Input provisioning (seed, fertiliser)
    • On-site storage and aggregation
    • Soil testing and basic diagnostics
    • Digital solutions for farm management
    • Farmer training and data collection
    • Value addition services
    • Logistics coordination

    02 — The System Vision

    Level 2: Farmhub Ops Centre

    Coordinating Regional Operations

    A master franchise entity that oversees, enables, and standardises a network of Farmhub Field Stations across a region or country.
    It leverages our Farmhub Playbook and acts as the coordination layer providing:
    • Standardised operating protocols
    • Franchise support and compliance
    • Logistics coordination
    • Regional marketing
    • Performance monitoring
    • Policy engagement
    It's the operating layer that makes a scalable, coordinated, and financially viable Farmhub ecosystem possible.

    02 — The System Vision

    Level 3: Farmhub Mission Control

    Enabling System-wide Standardisation

    A platform and backbone of the Farmhub system that manages shared, high-cost assets and protocols required for ecosystem scaling.
    It does not operate Farmhubs — it enables others to operate through:
    • Capital-intensive assets via PAYG
    • Farmhub OS digital platform
    • Governance infrastructure
    • Performance data systems
    • Financial visibility tools
    • Investment structuring
    Think of it as the infrastructure grid, software stack, and financial rail combined — built to serve distributed, regenerative rural economies.

    02 — The System Vision

    Bridging What’s Broken

    Creating a pathway for resources to do their job

    Africa is not short on agricultural potential. The resources exist — funding, tools, knowldge, infrastructure, and data — but the delivery architecture does not
    Today, the system is incredibly disjointed:
    • Inputs are fragmented across multiple players.
    • Farmers are unsupported, operating alone.
    • Institutions and capital struggle to coordinate at ground level.
    • This isn’t a problem of scarcity — it’s a problem of system design.
    The Farmhub model introduces the missing architecture
    By connecting upstream resources with farming communities through an integrated three-layer system — infrastructure, coordination, and service delivery — Farmhub transforms fragmentation into flow. It stitches together what’s siloed, creating a clear, scalable pathway for value to move efficiently from funders and enablers to the field — and back. This is not just a delivery system. It’s a new connective tissue for agricultural transformation at scale

    02 — The System Strategy

    PropCo, OpCo & Franchise OpCo

    One System, Three Roles: How We Structure for Scale

    To scale across countries and contexts — and to align with different investment types, risk appetites, and operational needs — we separate the Farmhub model into three distinct roles.
    Each role is carried out by a different kind of entity:
    • Level 3: Mission Control — owns and invests in the infrastructure (PropCo)
    • Level 2: Ops Centre — operates and manages the system in each country (OpCo)
    • Level 1: Field Station — delivers value directly to farmers (Franchise OpCo)
    This separation of roles lets us:
    • Protect infrastructure assets while deploying them where needed
    • Maintain operational excellence through local partners
    • Scale trust and accountability through standardization

    03 — The System Strategy

    Level 1: Farmhub Field Station

    Franchise OpCo: Service Fees

    The Level 1 "Farmhub Field Station" is a franchisee-operated local hub, generating revenue from core agri-services to Smallholder Farmers.
    Services that are monetised at unit level:
    -
  • Mechanisation-as-a-service (per hectare/hour basis)
  • Input resale margin (bulk-to-retail markup)
  • Post-harvest handling and aggregation fees
  • Optional: paid advisory packages
    • Key factors:
    • Low upfront financing costs for the franchisee
    • Franchisee pays fixed % royalty fee to National Hub
    • Breakeven at 700–1,000 farmers served
    • Target: ~1,500 farmers within 18–24 months/one field station

    03 — The System Strategy

    Level 2: Farmhub Ops Centre

    OpCo: Franchise Fees

    The Level 2 Ops Centre is a master franchise model that governs quality, supports operations, and earns from system growth. It is the critical link between the Field Stations and Mission Control.
    Core revenue streams:
    • Franchise licensing fees from each Farmhub node
    • Optional equity stake in high-performing Farmhubs
    • Coordination contracts with governments/donors
    Key factors:
    • Low fixed-cost, high-leverage business model
    • Built around enabling scale, not direct service delivery
    • Structured to serve 50–500 Farmhubs per country

    03 — The System Strategy

    Level 3: Farmhub Mission Control

    PropCo: Infrastructure-as-a-Service

    The Level 3 Farmhub entity is a platform + infrastructure holding company that earns from leasing, licensing, and data-layer monetisation.
    Core revenue streams:
    • Leasing income from equipment and storage units
    • Platform licensing to National Hubs and governments
    • Data monetisation & access services
    Key factors:
    • Structured for blended capital (equity + concessional finance)
    • Long-term value through asset ownership
    • Aggregates ecosystem insights for continuous innovation
    • Doesn't operate — enables trusted operation at scale

    03 — The System Strategy

    The Farmhub Playbook

    IP retained at Mission Control level to drive the system

    At the heart of the Farmhub model is a living, evolving playbook — a practical blueprint that defines how each part of the system works, and how it scales. This isn’t just a manual — it’s the IP backbone that powers a distributed, high-integrity franchise network
    • It standardises how services are delivered to smallholder farmers
    • It encodes best practices, workflows, compliance protocols, and partner models
    • And it ensures every Farmhub can deliver consistent, high-quality outcomes
    But the Playbook is more than documentation. It is coupled to the Farmhub OS — our digital platform. This means every update, insight, or adaptation flows straight from field to system… and back again. As the network grows, the Playbook grows with it — enabling continuous improvement, training, and replication at scale. It is how we maintain trust, integrity, and impact — across thousands of hubs and millions of farmers.

    Utilising Appropriate Scale Technology

    The lessons we have learned from Asia?

    Farmhub adapts successful appropriate-scale mechanisation models from Asia to meet the needs of smallholder farmers in Africa, focusing on equipment and service models designed for 1–5 hectare farms—typical in both regions.

    It deploys proven technologies like 50hp tractors, multi-crop threshers, precision seeders, and affordable irrigation systems. Delivered through a custom hiring service, these solutions lower barriers-to-entry by providing access without requiring ownership.

    Farmhub carefully adapts technologies to African conditions, considering local soils, cropping systems (teff, wheat, maize, sorghum), labour patterns, and infrastructure constraints

    To pilot this approach, we have established partnerships with Indian equipment manufacturers and Punjab Agricultural University, India's leading agricultural institution.

      01-The Context

      Smallholder Farmers are the Front Line…

      Smallholder farmers are the foundation of Africa's food system —yet they're the least supported. They're expected to grow food for the continent with no infrastructure, no finance, no market visibility, and no reliable services.

      This as a result of various structural problems and their effects
      • Manual farming →Low productivity
      • Informal markets → Unstable prices
      • No access to finance → No way to upgrade/grow
      • Highly climate-exposed → No buffers
      If we can't support the needs at the base of the system, nothing above it will function.

      01-The Context

      The Ecosystem struggles to support the Farmers…

      The supply chain of actors meant to support these farmers — mechanisation service providers, input distributors, aggregators, buyers — are fragmented, undercapitalised, and operating without standardisation or scale. They also face structural problems that limit their ability to create impact and support the system:

      • Services are fragmented→ Informal and often donor-dependent
      • No interoperable platform→ No standard business model to scale
      • No risk pooling→ No systemic way to pool risk or create shared infrastructure
      • Ad hoc markets→ Markets are spotty, and aggregation is ad hoc
      This service ecosystem is the engine room for scale — but everyone is building in isolation → no compounding value.

      01-The Context

      Government struggles to support the Ecosystem

      Governments are expected to deliver food security, rural job creation, and climate resilience. But they're trapped managing an informal sector that's hard to track, fund, or govern.

      • Inefficient investment→ Most public programs lack durability and fail to create systemic change
      • Siloed data→ Can't design or adapt policy fast enough without integrated systems
      • Foreign input dependence→ Over-reliance on imports leads to loss of food sovereignty
      • Pilot overload→ Too many isolated initiatives, not enough systemic solutions
      Governments need investment-ready systems they can trust and align with — not more one-off projects.

      02 — The System Vision

      Level 1: Farmhub Field Station

      Structuring & Servicing the Last Mile

      A Farmhub Field Station is a local service hub that provides smallholder farmers with access to the core infrastructure they need to close the yield, income, and market gap.
      Each hub is a physical location — franchised and locally operated — offering affordable, standardised access to:
      • Mechanisation-as-a-service (e.g. tractor hire)
      • Input provisioning (seed, fertiliser, tools)
      • On-site storage and aggregation
      • Soil testing and basic diagnostics
      • Digital solutions for farm management
      • Farmer training and data collection
      • Value addition services
      • Logistics coordination
      The Farmhub is the rural service hub for smallholder agriculture — like a franchise field station that delivers yield, tools, and trust

      02 — The System Vision

      Level 2: Farmhub Ops Centre

      Coordinating Regional Operations

      Farmhub Mission Control is the master franchise entity that oversees, enables, and standardises a network of Farmhub Field Stations across a region or country.
      It leverages our Farmhub Playbook and acts as the coordination layer providing:
      • Standardised operating protocols
      • Franchise support and compliance
      • Logistics coordination
      • Regional marketing
      • Performance monitoring
      • Policy engagement
      It's the operating layer that makes a scalable, coordinated, and financially viable Farmhub ecosystem possible.

      02 — The System Vision

      Level 3: Farmhub Mission Control

      Enabling System-wide Standardisation

      Farmhub Infrastructure is the platform and backbone of the Farmhub system that manages shared, high-cost assets and protocols required for ecosystem scaling.
      It does not operate Farmhubs — it enables others to operate through:
      • Capital-intensive assets via PAYG
      • Farmhub OS digital platform
      • Governance infrastructure
      • Performance data systems
      • Financial visibility tools
      • Investment structuring
      Think of it as the infrastructure grid, software stack, and financial rail combined — built to serve distributed, regenerative rural economies.

      02 — The System Vision

      Bridging What’s Broken

      Creating a pathway for resources to do their job

      Africa is not short on agricultural potential. The resources exist — funding, tools, training, infrastructure, and data — but the delivery architecture does not
      Today, the system is incredibly disjointed:
      • Inputs are fragmented across multiple players.
      • Farmers are unsupported, operating alone.
      • Institutions and capital struggle to coordinate at ground level.
      • This isn’t a problem of scarcity — it’s a problem of system design.
      The Farmhub model introduces the missing architecture
      By connecting upstream resources with last-mile farming communities through an integrated three-layer system — infrastructure, coordination, and service delivery — Farmhub transforms fragmentation into flow. It stitches together what’s siloed, creating a clear, scalable pathway for value to move efficiently from funders and enablers to the field — and back. This is not just a delivery system. It’s a new connective tissue for agricultural transformation at scale

      PropCo, OpCo & Franchise OpCo

      One System, Three Roles: How We Structure for Scale

      To scale across countries and contexts — and to align with different investment types, risk appetites, and operational needs — we separate the Farmhub model into three distinct roles.
      Each role is carried out by a different kind of entity:
      • Level 3: Mission Control — owns and invests in the infrastructure (PropCo)
      • Level 2: Ops Centre — operates and manages the system in each country (OpCo)
      • Level 1: Field Station — delivers value directly to farmers (Franchise OpCo)
      This separation of roles lets us:
      • Protect infrastructure assets while deploying them where needed
      • Maintain operational excellence through local partners
      • Scale trust and accountability through standardization

      03 — The System Strategy

      Level 2: Farmhub Ops Centre

      OpCo: Franchise Fees

      The Level 2 Ops Centre is a master franchise model that governs quality, supports operations, and earns from system growth. It is the critical link between the Field Stations and Mission Control.
      Core revenue streams:
      • Franchise licensing fees from each Farmhub node
      • Optional equity stake in high-performing Farmhubs
      • Coordination contracts with governments/donors
      Key factors:
      • Low fixed-cost, high-leverage business model
      • Built around enabling scale, not direct service delivery
      • Structured to serve 50–500 Farmhubs per country

      03 — The System Strategy

      Level 3: Farmhub Mission Control

      PropCo: Infrastructure-as-a-Service

      The Level 3 Farmhub entity is a platform + infrastructure holding company that earns from leasing, licensing, and data-layer monetisation.
      Core revenue streams:
      • Leasing income from equipment and storage units
      • Platform licensing to National Hubs and governments
      • Data monetisation & access services
      Key factors:
      • Structured for blended capital (equity + concessional finance)
      • Long-term value through asset ownership
      • Aggregates ecosystem insights for continuous innovation
      • Doesn't operate — enables trusted operation at scale

      03 — The System Strategy

      The Farmhub Playbook

      IP retained at Mission Control level to drive the system

      At the heart of the Farmhub model is a living, evolving playbook — a practical blueprint that defines how each part of the system works, and how it scales. This isn’t just a manual — it’s the IP backbone that powers a distributed, high-integrity franchise network
      • It standardises how services are delivered to smallholder farmers
      • It encodes best practices, workflows, compliance protocols, and partner models
      • And it ensures every Farmhub — whether in Kenya, Uganda, or Ethiopia — can deliver consistent, high-quality outcomes
      But the Playbook is more than documentation. It is coupled to the Farmhub OS — our digital platform. This means every update, insight, or adaptation flows straight from field to system… and back again. As the network grows, the Playbook grows with it — enabling continuous improvement, training, and replication at scale. It is how we maintain trust, integrity, and impact — across thousands of hubs and millions of farmers.

      Utilising Appropriate Scale Technology

      The lessons we have learned from Asia?

      Farmhub adapts successful appropriate-scale mechanisation models from Asia to meet the needs of smallholder farmers in Africa, focusing on equipment and service models designed for 1–5 hectare farms—typical in both regions.

      It deploys proven technologies like 50hp tractors, multi-crop threshers, precision seeders, and affordable irrigation systems. Delivered through a custom hiring service, these solutions lower barriers-to-entry by providing access without requiring ownership.

      Farmhub carefully adapts technologies to African conditions, considering local soils, cropping systems (teff, wheat, maize, sorghum), labour patterns, and infrastructure constraints

      To pilot this approach, we have established partnerships with Indian equipment manufacturers and Punjab Agricultural University, India's leading agricultural institution.
